The Bag Audit

You get fourteen slots and the rules do not care how much you paid for any of them. This works out which clubs are actually earning their place, which two are quietly doing the same job, and which distance you have no club for at all. Runs entirely in your browser — nothing uploaded, nothing sold to you.

02 · How The Audit Works

Fourteen slots, and no sentimentality

Most golfers do not choose fourteen clubs. They accumulate them. A set gets bought, a driver gets upgraded, a hybrid arrives at Christmas, and the club that got replaced never quite leaves the bag. This is the audit nobody performs on themselves.

Dead wood score

Uses per round multiplied by trust out of five. A driver you hit twelve times and rate 4 scores 48. A 3-wood you hit once and rate 2 scores 2. Anything scoring 4 or less is a passenger — and a club you never take out at all is not a club, it is luggage. It is deliberately crude, because the honest version of this question only needs two inputs.

Gapping

Your clubs get sorted longest to shortest and the distance between each pair is measured. Over 20 yards is a hole you have no club for. Under 8 yards is two clubs doing one job, which means one of your fourteen slots is being wasted on a spare. Between 10 and 15 is where you want to live.

Why the gaps matter more than the gear

A 20-yard hole in your set does not show up as a bad shot. It shows up as a decision you cannot make properly — a club you have to hit soft or force, several times a round, every round. That costs more over a season than whichever driver you are being told to buy next.

The honest limitation

This audit only knows what you tell it, and golfers are famously generous about their own carry distances. If your numbers come from the one you caught flush downwind, the ladder will look tidier than your bag actually is. Use carry, not total, and use the number you hit on a normal day.
