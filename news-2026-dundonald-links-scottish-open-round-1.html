Firm Fairways, Broken Scorecards: Dundonald's Wild Day One

Bone-dry fairways turned Dundonald Links into a minefield. Two players tamed it. One major champion did not. Here's how round one unfolded.

BY GOLFRAW EDITORIAL PUBLISHED: THU 23 JUL 2026
The Quick Verdict

Dundonald Links chewed up most of the field on day one of the Scottish Open, and nobody got chewed up worse than Minjee Lee. While Lauren Coughlin and Jenny Shin carded matching 66s to share the lead, the Australian major winner limped home with a 13-over 85 that left her tied for second-last in the entire field.

What happened in round one?

Coughlin, the defending champion from 2024, and South Korea's Jenny Shin both fired six-under 66s to grab a share of the clubhouse lead. Both players started their rounds on the back nine. Both took full advantage of a course playing nothing like its usual self, courtesy of a recent heat wave across Europe that's left the turf firm, fast, and unforgiving off the tee.

Coughlin and Shin found the right side of a brutal course

Coughlin knows Dundonald better than most, having won here back in 2024, but even she wasn't prepared for how differently the course played this time around. Eight birdies against two bogeys got her to 66, but she was quick to point out the conditions had nothing in common with previous visits.

"It was pretty difficult out there," Coughlin said. "I've not ever really played it when it's been this firm, especially off the tee, so quite a few different things off the tee than normal where I used to be able to hit the driver a lot of places."

Shin, playing the same nine-hole start as Coughlin, leaned into the firmness rather than fighting it. Seven birdies on her card included birdies at each of her first three par-5s, and she credited the drier ground for turning normally unreachable holes into scoring chances.

"It's playing a little shorter than the years past when it was quite wet out here," Shin explained. "And the fact I was able to reach the par-5s and take advantage of making the birdies there, and having a few 58-degree wedges coming into the green, helped. I was able to get my ball within 15 feet and when I did have the chance for birdie, I did make it. So it was a good day."

But it gets messier further down the leaderboard

Here's the thing about a course running fast and firm: it rewards precision and absolutely punishes anyone who's slightly off. Minjee Lee found that out the hard way. Seven bogeys and three double-bogeys added up to a 13-over 85, a genuinely brutal number for a multiple major champion, and it left her rooted near the bottom of the entire field.

A Lim Kim sits just one shot off the pace at 67, while Sweden's Lisa Pettersson was the only other player in the field to break 70, carding a 69. Everyone else found Dundonald considerably less forgiving.

What does this mean for the big names?

World No. 1 Nelly Korda didn't exactly light it up either, posting a 72 to sit tied for 14th after round one. She was grouped with defending event champion Lottie Woad of England, who stumbled early with a double bogey at the third and a bogey at the fourth before steadying the ship for a 74.

The Australian contingent had a mixed day across the board. Major winners Grace Kim and Hannah Green shared the best result among their countrywomen with matching 74s, good for a tie for 33rd, sitting two shots clear of Cassie Porter. Scottish-born Karis Davidson and Robyn Choi both carded 76s, keeping the theme of the day intact: nobody found this course easy, and the ones who did stood out immediately on the leaderboard.

With conditions this unforgiving and a gap already opening up near the top, round two at Dundonald looks set to separate the field even further before the weekend even arrives.
