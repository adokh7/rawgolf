Scottie Scheffler Won't Pick a Side in the DeChambeau-McIlroy Feud — But He Just Defended Bryson Anyway

Scottie Scheffler refuses to take sides in the Bryson DeChambeau-Rory McIlroy cold war, but he's not staying quiet about the Open Championship penalty that started it all. Here's what the world No. 1 actually said.

BY GOLFRAW EDITORIAL PUBLISHED: WED 22 JUL 2026
The Quick Verdict

Rory McIlroy has made no secret of his frustration with Bryson DeChambeau, and the fallout from DeChambeau's two-stroke penalty at Royal Birkdale hasn't helped. Scottie Scheffler, who gets along fine with both men, was asked to weigh in on the drama ahead of the 3M Open — and while he refused to name names, he made it clear he thinks golf's culture of personal rivalry is overrated, and that DeChambeau got a raw deal from the cameras, not from his character.

Golf's Odd Man Out on Manufactured Rivalries

Scheffler has a good relationship with both DeChambeau, his Ryder Cup teammate, and McIlroy, who hasn't hidden that he's "not particularly fond" of DeChambeau since their pairing at the 2025 Masters. Speaking to reporters before the 3M Open outside Minneapolis, Scheffler was asked point-blank whether the McIlroy-DeChambeau tension — and the media attention it generates — is actually good for the sport.

His answer cut against the grain of how most athletes talk about rivalries. Scheffler argued that golf simply doesn't work the way basketball or other head-to-head sports do, where you're physically trying to stop an opponent in real time.

"I could never stop somebody from doing anything in golf," Scheffler said. "I felt like when I played basketball, there would always be rivalries because you're going to play defense against a person and that is much more personal... but in golf, we're always competing against ourselves on the golf course."

Notably, Scheffler never mentioned DeChambeau or McIlroy by name throughout his comments, keeping the answer philosophical rather than personal.

The Penalty That Started It All

The tension traces back to the 154th Open Championship at Royal Birkdale, where DeChambeau was hit with a two-stroke penalty on the fifth hole during his second round after officials ruled he'd improved his lie in the thick fescue. Chief referee Grant Moir called it an "inadvertent improving" of the swing area, explaining that the rule applies "even when the action is accidental". Video showed DeChambeau stepping through the long grass and planting his foot directly behind the ball, flattening the vegetation in a way that appeared to clear his backswing.

DeChambeau didn't take it quietly. He threatened to withdraw from the tournament, marched to the R&A offices, and later got heckled with shouts of "you're a cheat" on the 18th green during Saturday's third round. The penalty turned his second-round 66 into a 68 and ultimately cost him more than £200,000 in prize money — the difference between a share of sixth place and where he actually finished.

Scheffler Draws a Clear Line

Asked directly about the penalty after his own final round at Birkdale, Scheffler didn't dodge the question — he defended DeChambeau's integrity while still acknowledging the rules process could be tightened up.

"I think maybe we need to be a little bit more strict in the way that we are policing guys, but it's a little bit of a balance because you've got certain guys like a Bryson who every shot he hits is going to be shown on TV," Scheffler said. "When you have camera angles that are far away, things can look different than they do when you're right there."

He then went further, drawing on years of history with DeChambeau to push back on the cheating label altogether.

"All I'll say is Bryson, I've known him for a long time, and he's a lot of things, but he's definitely not a cheater," Scheffler said. "I've never seen him cheat once, whether we were having a putting contest on the putting green or chipping contest, or anytime we played amateur golf, I never saw anything suspicious. When you're watching him there from that camera angle, maybe he was a touch careless around his ball, but I don't think he did anything intentionally."

The Raw Take

Scheffler's answer here is smarter than it looks on the surface. By refusing to name McIlroy or DeChambeau while still weighing in on the actual rules question, he avoided getting dragged into a personality war he clearly has zero interest in — and made a genuinely interesting point about why golf doesn't manufacture rivalries the way contact sports do. You can't guard Bryson DeChambeau. You can only out-shoot him.

But don't mistake the diplomacy for fence-sitting on the substance. Scheffler drew a firm line: he thinks the officiating needs sharper teeth given how every camera angle turns a routine walk through the rough into a controversy, and he's not remotely buying that DeChambeau cheated on purpose. Coming from the No. 1 player in the world, who has no obligation to stick his neck out for a guy who isn't even his closest friend on tour, that's a meaningful vote of confidence — and it says more about DeChambeau's standing in the locker room than any heckle on the 18th green ever will.
