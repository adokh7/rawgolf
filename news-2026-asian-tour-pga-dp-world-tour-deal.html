The Asian Tour Is Back in the Room—and LIV Just Lost Some Air

The Asian Tour just gained a path to bigger stages. Here’s what the PGA TOUR-DP World Tour alliance really changes.

BY GOLFRAW Editorial PUBLISHED: WED 22 JUL 2026
The Quick Verdict

The Asian Tour, DP World Tour and PGA TOUR have agreed to a multi-year partnership running through at least 2029, creating a formal route for top Asian Tour players to reach the DP World Tour and HotelPlanner Tour from 2027. The agreement also brings Asian Tour and DP World Tour co-sanctioned events back onto the calendar, with at least two scheduled in each of the 2027, 2028 and 2029 seasons.

This is more than another corporate handshake in a blazer-filled hotel ballroom. It gives ambitious players in Asia a clearer route upward—and it tightens the PGA TOUR and DP World Tour’s grip on the part of the global game that LIV Golf had worked hard to influence.

What does the new Asian Tour deal do?

The alliance gives high-performing Asian Tour members defined opportunities to progress to either the DP World Tour or the HotelPlanner Tour, beginning with the 2027 DP World Tour season. The tours have not yet announced the precise number of cards, eligibility cutoffs or final qualifying details; those are expected later in 2026.

That lack of detail is important. Do not let anyone sell you a finished product when the nuts and bolts are still in the box.

Still, the direction is clear. A player who builds a strong season on the Asian Tour will no longer be chasing vague promises of “international exposure.” There will be a recognised ladder toward Europe’s top circuit and its secondary tour. From there, the existing PGA TOUR-DP World Tour Strategic Alliance offers further opportunity: the top 10 eligible DP World Tour players can earn PGA TOUR cards for the following season.

For a young player in South Korea, Japan, Thailand, India, China or anywhere else across the region, that changes the career math. The road remains difficult. It is supposed to be difficult. But it is no longer quite so foggy.

Why are co-sanctioned events returning?

The DP World Tour and Asian Tour will co-sanction at least two events in each season from 2027 through 2029. Specific tournaments have not been confirmed, but the Hong Kong Open is among the events reported to be positioned to benefit from the renewed relationship.

Co-sanctioning is not a decorative label. It can mean better fields, wider ranking opportunities, stronger commercial value and tournament weeks that matter beyond one domestic market.

The two tours have history. Their previous formal alliance began in 2021, although their relationship stretches back to the 1999 Malaysian Open. So this is not a blind date. It is a reunion, with the PGA TOUR now sitting at the table and making sure the seating plan suits its broader strategy.

What does it mean for Asian Tour players?

It means performance on the Asian Tour can now lead to a clearer, formalised route into larger DP World Tour opportunities. That is the practical result—not a slogan about “global growth.”

For too long, professional golf outside the United States and Europe could feel like a maze built by people who enjoyed watching players get lost. A talented golfer could win in one region and still spend years piecing together starts, sponsor invitations and one-off exemptions somewhere else.

This deal does not abolish that struggle. It does make the route more legible.

The real winners will be the players who use the opportunity before it becomes familiar. Every newly created pathway eventually gets crowded. The first wave sees the opening. The next wave sees traffic.

Is this a blow to LIV Golf?

Yes, strategically. The agreement reduces the Asian Tour’s value as a potential development or event platform for LIV Golf, although it does not eliminate LIV from Asia or settle professional golf’s larger civil war.

LIV has used Asian Tour events as a useful part of its broader ecosystem. This new alignment makes that relationship harder to weaponise. The Asian Tour is now formally tied into the PGA TOUR-DP World Tour structure, including pathways, co-sanctioned events and commercial collaboration.

That matters because professional golf is not only about purses and broadcasts. It is about access: who can offer players a route, who can give events legitimacy, and who owns the next generation before that generation becomes expensive.

The PGA TOUR and DP World Tour are not merely defending tournaments here. They are trying to control the pipeline.

Why did the tours do this now?

The partnership consolidates the PGA TOUR and DP World Tour’s global structure at a time when the sport is still fractured by the battle over players, tournaments and influence. The deal was confirmed after discussions during the 2026 Open Championship at Royal Birkdale.

The official language is about cooperation, commercial opportunity and talent development. Fine. Those are real things.

But professional golf is also a business fight with scorecards. The PGA TOUR and DP World Tour have spent years building their Strategic Alliance. Bringing the Asian Tour inside that structure makes the alliance less regional and more difficult to ignore.

Christian Hardy, the PGA TOUR’s senior vice president of international, said the three tours share a vision of a more connected global game, with new chances for players and stronger development routes. That is the public version.

The raw version is simpler: if you own more of the road, fewer rivals can charge tolls on it.

The Raw Take

This is good news for players. A golfer who performs in Asia deserves a route that does not depend on luck, favours or whether somebody in Florida remembers his name.

It is also a calculated power move. The PGA TOUR and DP World Tour did not wake up one morning and discover global unity. They saw an opening, saw the importance of Asia, and made sure LIV Golf had less room to operate there.

Everybody gets to call it progress. The player who earns a better route probably will.
