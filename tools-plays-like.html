02 · The Numbers Behind It

Why your shot is not playing its yardage

The sprinkler head says 150. The sprinkler head does not know it is 4°C, blowing 15 into your face, and the green sits 20 feet above you. Four separate forces are moving that number, and amateurs routinely ignore all of them and then blame the strike.

Cold air and a cold ball

Roughly 2 yards per 10°F below a 70°F baseline. A cold golf ball is less elastic and cold air is denser, so you lose carry twice over. At near freezing you can be a full club down before you have done anything wrong.

Wind is not symmetrical

A headwind hurts roughly twice as much as a tailwind helps: about 1 yard per mph into it, around half a yard per mph downwind. That asymmetry is why "it is only a two-club wind" is almost always an underestimate when you are hitting into it.

Altitude is free distance

Thin air means about 2% extra carry per 1,000 feet. In Denver at 5,280 feet a 150-yard shot plays roughly 16 yards shorter. This is why holiday golf in the mountains briefly convinces people they have found something.

The humidity myth

Here is the one everybody gets backwards. Humid air feels heavy, so golfers assume it is denser. It is not. A water molecule is lighter than the nitrogen and oxygen it displaces, so humid air is marginally less dense and the ball flies a fraction further. The effect is small enough to ignore entirely — but it is certainly not costing you distance.
