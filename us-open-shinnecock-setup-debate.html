Did the USGA Take the Teeth Out of Shinnecock? The U.S. Open Setup Debate, Told Straight

Did the USGA take the teeth out of Shinnecock? The U.S. Open setup debate told straight: why the greens were slowed, why fans are mad, and the honest verdict.

BY S. OKAFOR PUBLISHED SAT 20 JUN

After one round of the 2026 U.S. Open, a chunk of golf fans had already made up their minds: the USGA chickened out. They softened the greens, slowed them to a crawl, and turned the meanest course in American golf into a pushover. The word getting thrown around was that Shinnecock had lost its teeth.

It is a fair thing to be annoyed about. It is also only half the story. The truth is more interesting, and it puts the USGA in a no-win spot that has very little to do with cowardice. Here is the setup debate, told straight, with both sides on the table.

What the USGA Actually Did

Let us start with the facts, because the anger skips past them.

Ahead of the tournament, the USGA made three clear moves to soften Shinnecock. They slowed the greens down to 10.5 on the Stimpmeter, which is the slowest a U.S. Open has played in over 30 years. They syringed, or lightly watered, the greens every day to keep the turf alive. And they set conservative, gentler hole locations.

Why? One word: wind. The forecast called for gusts that could top 30 mph, with low humidity drying everything out. On fast, firm greens in that kind of wind, golf balls do not just roll. They move on their own, refusing to sit still even after a player has addressed them. That is not a tough test. That is a broken one. Nobody, not the players, not the fans, not the USGA, wants to watch balls oscillating on the greens and players unable to mark a putt.

So the USGA built in a cushion. They aimed for green speeds well below what they ran at Oakmont a year earlier, on purpose, to keep the course playable when the wind howled.

Why They Were So Cautious: The Ghosts of 2004 and 2018

To understand the caution, you have to understand the scars. Shinnecock has burned the USGA before, badly, and twice.

In 2004, the leaders were six under through 36 holes, and the USGA decided the course was too easy. So they stopped watering the greens before the weekend. The wind did the rest, sucking every drop of moisture out of the ground until the greens turned glassy and unfair. The seventh hole became so absurd they had to hand-water it mid-round just to keep it playable. Tiger Woods flatly said the USGA had lost control of a great golf course.

Then in 2018, it happened again. The setup got away from them on Saturday, with wind and dry conditions pushing some hole locations past the line of fairness, and the USGA had to publicly admit they went too far.

Two visits, two embarrassments, both caused by pushing a fragile course too hard in the wind. So when the 2026 forecast looked nasty, the USGA did the obvious thing. They erred on the safe side. After being roasted for making Shinnecock too brutal, they were never going to risk a third disaster. Can you blame them?

The Case for the Critics: It Did Look Soft

Now, the other side, because the fans fuming are not wrong about everything.

It did look soft. Players themselves said so. One described the greens as weirdly, oddly soft, not at all how he remembered Shinnecock playing. And here is the part that genuinely backfired: the USGA watered the greens between the morning and afternoon waves to brace for big winds, and then the heavy winds did not fully arrive in the afternoon. The fog delay pushed the late starters into calmer evening conditions on already-softened greens.

The result was Wyndham Clark carving up the course for a record 64. The best players in the world, on a U.S. Open Sunday-toughness venue, suddenly throwing darts at receptive greens. For a championship that sells itself as the hardest test in golf, watching someone go six under in the opening round feels wrong to a lot of fans. They tune in to see the world's best suffer, not stroll.

So the criticism has a real core. The USGA prepared for a storm, the storm underdelivered, and the safety cushion turned into a scoring free-for-all for the lucky afternoon group.

The Part Everyone Forgets

Here is the detail that cools the outrage down, though. The course was not actually a pushover.

Even with the slowed greens, the morning wave, the players who got the wind, still played nearly four shots over par on average. Four over. That is hard. That is a brutal, grinding test of golf. The carnage was real for half the field.

So the story is not really "the USGA made Shinnecock easy." The real story is "the draw made Shinnecock a tale of two courses." The morning players got hammered by the wind. The afternoon players caught a break when it died down and feasted on softer greens. The setup was the same for everyone. The weather split the field in half, and that is mostly luck, not USGA failure.

When you only look at Clark's 64 and scream that the teeth are gone, you are ignoring the dozens of players who got chewed up that same morning.

The Real Problem: Shinnecock Is a No-Win Course

Step back and you see the actual issue, and it is bigger than one round of grumbling.

Shinnecock puts the USGA in an impossible spot. Most U.S. Open venues let you dial difficulty up or down with a margin for error. Shinnecock does not. The course is so exposed to wind, with so little to slow it down, that the line between tough and broken is razor thin. You cannot just slow the greens and pick easy pins to play it safe, because the gentle hole locations barely exist on these wild, tilted greens. But the moment you push toward fast and firm, one shift in the wind can tip the whole thing into the unfair chaos of 2004 and 2018.

In other words, at most courses the USGA holds the cards. At Shinnecock, the weather always holds one more. You can set it up perfectly on paper and still watch nature wreck it, or play it safe and watch a calm evening make it look soft. There is no clean win here.

So this week they chose the safe side. Given their history at this exact venue, that is a defensible call, even if the opening round made them look timid.

The Raw Read

Here is the honest verdict, and it refuses to fully take either side.

The fans are right that Round 1 looked soft, and the watering-then-no-wind sequence was an unforced bit of bad luck that handed the afternoon wave a gift. A record 64 at a U.S. Open should raise eyebrows.

But the fans are wrong that the USGA got scared and ruined the championship. They made a reasonable, scarred-by-history decision to protect a fragile course from a forecast that genuinely threatened to break it. And the proof that they did not gut the place is simple: the morning half of the field still got beaten up by nearly four shots over par.

The deeper truth is that Shinnecock is one of the only courses on earth that can humble the USGA itself. They are not fighting the players here. They are fighting the wind, and the wind does not read press conferences.

Three rounds remain, the forecast still has teeth, and this course has a long history of saving its cruelty for the weekend. Anyone calling Shinnecock toothless after one calm evening might want to wait. She tends to bite back when you least expect it.
