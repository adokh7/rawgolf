· "EXACTLY WHAT I WANTED": MacIntyre Shrugs Off a Drone to Open The Open With 67

BY GOLF RAW STAFF PUBLISHED THU 16 JUL

Quick Summary: A drone nearly wrecked Robert MacIntyre's first tee shot at Royal Birkdale. He backed off, glared at the sky, waited for someone to sort it, then ripped his drive down the fairway and made birdie. He signed for a 3-under 67 and called it exactly the round he came for, adopting a disciplined, driver-free strategy that should worry the rest of the field.

The Drone, and the Birdie That Followed

Deal with the nonsense, hit the shot, move on. Dawn at Birkdale, hundreds of fans round the first tee, MacIntyre introduced to a roar. He settles over the ball. Then a drone starts buzzing overhead, and he has to step away and gesture up at it, waiting while word gets passed along that he wants the thing gone.

There's a real edge of history to a Scot getting buzzed by a drone on the biggest stage, and he clearly felt it. But once it cleared off, he stepped back up and drilled one down the left of that narrow opening fairway, then rolled in the birdie putt. A shakier player lets that moment linger for three holes. MacIntyre turned it into a shot gained.

A 67 Built on Patience, Not Fireworks

Four birdies, one bogey, and almost no drama in between. What stands out on the card isn't any single shot, it's what MacIntyre didn't do. He left the driver in the bag on holes where the rest of the field reached for it, deciding that Birkdale's pot bunkers were a bigger threat than any yardage he'd give up.

"Sometimes I laid myself too far back but had to accept it and deal with a longer shot in," he said. That's the trade. Longer approaches, but out of the sand, and on a course this firm the sand is where good rounds go to die. He's sticking with the plan all week, and he sounded genuinely pleased with how boring it was. "Delighted. A very solid round of golf," he told BBC Radio 5 Live. He wasn't kidding about solid.

This Is Not the MacIntyre of 2019

Seventh Open, and he's a completely different animal now. The results were always decent, ties for sixth, seventh and eighth in previous years. What's changed is the belief, and he put it better than any pundit could.

"In 2019, I felt like I was just being part of the tournament. I was making up the numbers." Now? "If I play the golf I know I can play, I'm going to have a chance to win come Sunday, and that's the difference." He rolled in here off a strong Scottish Open, and links golf fits his game like almost nobody else's in the field. He's not hoping to hang around this week. He turned up expecting to contend.

So Is He a Real Threat at Birkdale?

Yes, and Thursday only sharpened that case. He shapes it, he flights it low, he grew up battling exactly the firm, breezy stuff Birkdale is dishing out, and the home crowd will drag him round all four days. The bookies had him among the favourites before a ball was struck, trailing only names like Scottie Scheffler, and nothing about that 67 argues otherwise.

The wind barely showed up on Thursday, though, and plenty of names went low before the course found its teeth. MacIntyre said himself he's not about to start chasing a number. A first major has felt like a when rather than an if for this guy for a while, though, and opening his first crack at Birkdale with a controlled 67 is how these weeks start. He's back out Friday at 12:25 BST.

The Raw Read

The score wasn't the dangerous bit. The satisfaction was. MacIntyre didn't hit a single highlight-reel shot on Thursday, and he walked off buzzing anyway, because the patient, slightly dull round he played was the exact one he'd drawn up in his head.

That's a bad sign for everyone else. Players who happily grind out pars on a mean links, who can leave the driver alone and the ego in the car park for four hours, are usually the ones still standing on Sunday. He wants the Claret Jug more than most men in this field, the course suits him, and it took a drone to even briefly rattle him. That is not a fun player to be chasing.
