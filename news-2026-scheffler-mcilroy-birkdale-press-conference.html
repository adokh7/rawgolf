· RAW TAKE: "We're All Going to Die": Scheffler and McIlroy Get Dark on Legacy

Ask the two best golfers alive about their legacy and you brace for the usual. Instead, days out from The Open 2026 at Royal Birkdale, Scottie Scheffler and Rory McIlroy both shrugged and started talking about being dead. It's the best press conference moment of the golf year.

BY GOLF RAW STAFF PUBLISHED WED 15 JUL 2026 · 12:04 UPDATED 12:04 4 MIN READ

Ask the two best golfers alive about their legacy and you brace for the usual. Honoured, humbled, blessed to be in the conversation. Instead, days out from The Open 2026 at Royal Birkdale, Scottie Scheffler and Rory McIlroy both shrugged and started talking about being dead.

Scheffler even called his own headline. It's the best press conference moment of the golf year, and it tells you plenty about where both men's heads are.

What Did Scottie Scheffler Say About Legacy?

That it does nothing for him, and he spelled out why. Asked if he plays for a place in history, Scheffler said no, not really. The Scottie Scheffler legacy question clearly bores him. Then he pushed further. "Like when I die: 'Hey, Scottie won four majors and 20 tournaments and he won this much money' — that has zero effect on me."

He kept walking into darker ground. "This is going to sound a little morbid," he said, "at the end of the day, I'm going to live my life, and it's going to end. When it ends, I'm going somewhere else, and I'm not going to be here anymore." Then he grinned and wrote the story for everyone. He asked whether last year's quote was "What's the point?" and this year's would be "we're all going to die." The Scheffler legacy comments basically headlined themselves.

What Did Rory McIlroy Say?

Same destination, different road. Asked if more trophies and records would shape how he's seen in a hundred years, McIlroy didn't blink. "I'll be long gone. I'll be dead," he said. "I don't think I'll be seeing what people say about me. I'll be six feet under. I don't think I'll be a ghost."

The McIlroy six feet under line grabbed the headlines, but the bit underneath mattered more. He called chasing records and results a "pretty unfulfilling pursuit," and owned that he'd learned it the hard way. "You have to enjoy the journey to get there," he said. "I've learned that the hard way at times by chasing results and records too much." From a man who spent over a decade tormented by a Masters he couldn't win, that reads as scar tissue, not a soundbite. His whole Rory McIlroy Open mindset now runs on it.

Why Does Scheffler Keep Getting Philosophical at The Open?

This championship pulls it out of him every year. It's a pattern now. Twelve months ago at Royal Portrush, days before he lifted his first Claret Jug, Scheffler gave a press conference people still quote. He admitted he wrestles daily with his own hunger to win, and openly asked whether it was worth it "to celebrate winning for a few minutes."

That "What's the point?" moment became one of the defining golf quotes of 2025. Then he went and won the thing. So the Scheffler press conference Open ritual is real. He turns up, questions the meaning of all of it, and plays some of his best golf. This year's take was calmer, more matter-of-fact, but the message held. History doesn't move him. He'd sooner be remembered for how he played than what he won.

Is Something Wrong With Scheffler?

No, and it's worth stating plainly. A man talking about death before a major looks like a warning sign at first glance. Read the full quote and it flips. Scheffler isn't miserable about golf. He's putting it in its place. He said he'd rather be remembered for the way he did it than the silverware, which is a healthy way for someone this dominant to think.

His faith sits underneath it. When he talks about going "somewhere else" after this life, that's a player whose meaning lives outside the game. For someone winning at a rate not seen since Tiger Woods, that anchor might be the reason the pressure never seems to flatten him. He plays loose because, to him, the golf was never the whole point.

Does This Matter for The Open?

More than the shrugging suggests, because both men are chasing history whether they admit caring or not. The Scheffler defend Open title storyline is huge: no one has retained the Claret Jug since Padraig Harrington in 2008. And McIlroy, already the sixth man to complete the career Grand Slam, would match the McIlroy Harry Vardon record as the European with the most men's majors if he wins at Birkdale. The very records they waved away are sitting right in front of them.

There's an edge to the mindset, too. A player who genuinely doesn't fear the history books is dangerous down the stretch, because he has nothing to lose. Both come in interesting. The Scheffler Scottish Open missed cut ended his first cut-free run in four years. McIlroy went T7 there and shot a closing 64, though he still snapped "I'm so bad at golf" mid-round. Two of the best ever, both a touch philosophical, both loaded for Royal Birkdale.

The Raw Read

There's something refreshing about the top two players on earth refusing to pretend the trophies are everything. Sport usually sells the opposite, all that talk of greatness and cementing your place among the immortals. Scheffler and McIlroy looked at it and said, more or less, we'll be dead, so who cares.

The clever twist is that the mindset probably makes them better. McIlroy spent years choking on records he wanted too badly and only broke through once he loosened his grip on them. Scheffler never carried that weight, and he's been the steadiest player of his era. Not caring about your legacy might be the healthiest way to build one. They'll both tee it up Thursday at the Open Championship 2026 Birkdale chasing a jug they swear won't matter when they're gone. Don't be shocked if one of them wins it because he believes exactly that in these major championships.
