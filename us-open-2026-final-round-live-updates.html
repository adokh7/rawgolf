LIVE UPDATES · U.S. Open 2026 Final Round: Wyndham Clark Pegged Back as the Field Charges

Updated through the front nine of the final round. Story developing.

BY GOLF RAW STAFF PUBLISHED SUN 21 JUN

Quick answer: Wyndham Clark started Sunday's final round at Shinnecock Hills with a six-shot lead, but the front nine got messy fast. A shaky start from Clark and a surprise charge — led not by Scottie Scheffler but by Sam Burns — cut the advantage to as little as one stroke before Clark's putter steadied him back to a handful. The wire-to-wire win is still his to lose, but for the first time all week, he is sweating.

Here is how the final round has unfolded so far, and why it suddenly got interesting.

How Big Was Clark's Lead, and What Happened?

Clark walked to the first tee with a six-shot cushion, one of the largest 54-hole leads in U.S. Open history, in company with names like Tiger Woods and Rory McIlroy. On paper, all he had to do was not fall apart.

Then he nearly started to. He opened with a bogey, gave another shot back at the second after missing the green and scrambling, and just like that the buffer that felt bulletproof on Saturday night began to leak. Shinnecock, firmer and meaner on Sunday as the USGA promised, started biting. The number that looked like a coronation turned into a genuine contest in about an hour.

Who Is Actually Challenging Clark?

Here is the surprise. The man making the real run is not Scottie Scheffler. It is Sam Burns.

Burns began the day seven shots back, an afterthought in most previews. Then he caught fire, birdieing three of his first five holes and climbing to 3-under, which at one stage trimmed Clark's lead to a single shot. For a few holes, the entire complexion of the championship flipped, and the runaway leader suddenly had a chaser breathing down his neck. A bogey at the ninth cooled Burns off slightly, but he served notice: this is not over.

That is the cruel beauty of a U.S. Open Sunday. Everyone watched Clark and Scheffler in the final group, and the threat came sneaking up from a player nobody was talking about.

Why Isn't Scheffler Making His Move?

Because the birthday fairy tale has not cooperated, at least not yet. Scheffler, playing on his 30th birthday with the career Grand Slam on the line, bogeyed the very first hole and has struggled to find the spark everyone expected. The crowd is desperate for him to charge, but the putts have not dropped and the chip-ins that lit up Saturday have not reappeared.

He is still out there, still capable of a back-nine surge, and a player of his caliber is never truly gone. But through the early holes, the man chasing history has been the quietest story in his own group. If anyone catches Clark, the evidence so far says it is more likely to be Burns than the world No. 1.

What Is the Crowd Doing?

Turning on Clark, openly. This has been the strange subplot of his entire week, and it boiled over Sunday.

The gallery sang happy birthday to Scheffler on the first tee, peppered Clark with heckles, and at least a few fans were ejected for going too far. There were shouts wishing him into bunkers and water, and a general sense of the whole place pulling against the leader.

To his credit, Clark has worn it. He has heard the jeers all week, kept marching, and answered trouble with clutch putts time and again. Playing the villain in front of a hostile crowd at a major is its own kind of pressure, and so far the noise has not broken him. Whether it wears on him over a long, tense back nine is one of the day's real questions.

Has Anyone Ever Lost a Six-Shot Final-Round Lead?

This is the stat hanging over everything. Per the Elias Sports Bureau, 21 players have taken a lead of six or more shots into the final round of a major. Twenty of them won. The only one who did not was Greg Norman, who melted down at the 1996 Masters and handed it to Nick Faldo.

There is an extra layer of history here, too. The last two times a 54-hole leader got caught at a Shinnecock U.S. Open, the man who got run down was Norman himself, in 1986 and 1995. So the ghosts of collapses past are very much in the Long Island air. The odds still say Clark closes it out. The history says it can happen, and Shinnecock is exactly the kind of place where it has.

What Else Has Happened on the Front Nine?

A few moments worth flagging from a tense afternoon:

  • Sahith Theegala's wild fourth. Buried in the thick stuff, he hacked out and somehow sent both his club flying over fans' heads and a second hidden ball popping out of the lie at the same time. He made a double bogey and tumbled from contention.
  • A Father's Day gift. Seventeen-year-old amateur Miles Russell, in his major debut, had his dad Joe take the bag on the 18th hole to caddie him in, a genuinely lovely moment on Father's Day. Russell also stuffed a wedge for birdie at the 16th to put a bow on his week.
  • Clark's putter, again. As he has all week, Clark keeps saving himself from the short and mid-range. Every time the round threatens to get away, he drains the putt that stops the bleeding. That stroke, more than anything, is why he is still in front.

The Raw Read

This is the round everyone wanted: a leader under real pressure, a hostile crowd, a firm and nasty Shinnecock, and a chaser nobody saw coming. Clark is still the favorite. He has the lead, the experience of closing out the 2023 U.S. Open, and a putter that refuses to quit. But for the first time all week, the outcome is genuinely in doubt, and the next nine holes will tell us whether this is a coronation or a collapse for the ages.

The smart money is still on Clark to land the plane. The whole point of a U.S. Open Sunday is that the plane does not always land. Stay with it.
