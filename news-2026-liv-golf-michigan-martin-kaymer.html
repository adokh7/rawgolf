Martin Kaymer Says Michigan Team Event Is All But Dead — And LIV's Bigger Problems Are Just Starting

Martin Kaymer says the LIV Golf Team Championships in Michigan are all but dead, with a September funding deadline and the loss of the Asian Tour throwing the league's 2027 future into serious doubt.

BY GOLFRAW EDITORIAL PUBLISHED: WED 22 JUL 2026
The Quick Verdict

Cleeks GC captain Martin Kaymer isn't sugarcoating it: the LIV Golf Team Championships at The Cardinal in Michigan look finished before they've started, with course infrastructure nowhere near ready and a formal call expected next Tuesday. That's the smaller headline. The bigger one is a September deadline to raise $350 million just to keep the league alive past this year, layered on top of the Asian Tour walking away from its alliance with LIV entirely.

A Captains' Meeting That Raised More Questions Than Answers

Kaymer sat in on a captains' meeting at LIV Golf UK this week, the league's first gathering since a seven-week competitive shutdown. That pause wasn't voluntary — LIV pulled the plug on its New Orleans regular-season stop in late June after heat and course-condition concerns got too loud to ignore, and the fallout from that decision is still working its way through the schedule.

Commissioner Scott O'Neil used the meeting to reassure players that outside money is circling the league, but Kaymer left with the same uncertainty he walked in with.

"We were hoping for a bit more information," Kaymer told reporters at JCB Golf & Country Club. "I mean, we got the information, but we were more looking for decisions or what was happening next year. And unfortunately, even Scott, he can't tell us because he doesn't know. It depends so much on the investors. Hopefully, I think there's a lot of interest and it's looking good. But we've all been there. If the signature is not there, then it doesn't mean anything."

Michigan Looks Finished, Indianapolis Might Take Its Place

Kaymer, LIV's Cleeks GC captain and a former world No. 1, put the odds of the Michigan event happening at roughly five percent, citing a course that simply doesn't have the infrastructure built yet to host it.

"I know that Indianapolis most likely is happening," Kaymer said. "There was a conversation yesterday. Michigan, highly unlikely. So basically, what we already knew. That's why I think it became a captains' meeting and not a players' meeting, because they couldn't give us much more than we had before."

There's chatter that Indianapolis could get repurposed as the makeshift team event if Michigan officially falls through, something Kaymer isn't thrilled about on principle.

"Of course, we are disappointed," Kaymer said. "I heard yesterday, actually, on the Golf Channel that there were talks that Indianapolis could become a team event instead of Michigan. That we don't know. I think that would be a shame that we get reduced even more individual tournaments. On the other hand, how do you want to have a team winning the whole season if you don't have a team event? We need to be honest. The whole season was a bit of a... there was so much happening. Cancelled New Orleans. Cancelled most likely the last event. So I think it's important to just finish the season and hopefully we'll have a plan for 2027."

A final ruling on Michigan is expected next Tuesday, though Kaymer wasn't optimistic based on what he'd heard the day before. "According to yesterday, it didn't look good," he said.

The Money Problem Nobody Can Solve Yet

The real story behind the schedule chaos is financial. LIV is chasing $350 million in fresh investment after Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund confirmed it's pulling its backing at the end of this season, and the league has a September deadline to lock down that funding or risk not operating at all in 2027.

Kaymer, a two-time major winner, made clear the team format still matters to him even as the bigger picture stays murky.

"It's just a shame for the whole LIV Golf schedule if it doesn't happen and for the whole team thing because we would love to play match play against each other," he said. "That would have been really cool. But if it doesn't happen, it is what it is and we just need to focus on hopefully 2027 and, you know, hit the reset button and try to be more independent next year."

Losing the Asian Tour Adds to the Pressure

LIV took another hit this week when its long-running partnership with the Asian Tour officially ended. The two sides had linked up back in 2021, before LIV even launched, with the Asian Tour offering a promotion-relegation pathway into the breakaway league. That relationship is now over — the Asian Tour has instead joined a global alliance with the PGA Tour and DP World Tour, leaving LIV without one of its few remaining institutional partners.

Kaymer didn't hide his frustration with how quickly the league's financial picture has unraveled since the start of the year.

"They are working their arses off to get a deal," he said. "But in the end, if those investors decide to pull out, then we don't play golf next year on LIV Golf. And if they decide to do it, then we have a chance."

Kaymer expects more clarity when LIV plays its penultimate regular-season event at Trump Bedminster in two weeks. Until then, the uncertainty is wearing on him.

"It's a bit sad because we all invested in this year and I'm not meaning financially," Kaymer said. "We invested time, emotions, hopes, the vision, and if it would fail that way, the way it failed now, because we've been told in February we are fully funded until 2031 and two months later, it all goes down. It would be a shame."

LIV Golf declined to comment on the status of the Michigan event, and The Cardinal course has also been approached.

The Raw Take

Strip away the diplomatic phrasing and what Kaymer is actually describing is a league running on fumes with no real answers to give its own players. A captains' meeting that produces nothing but "we don't know either" isn't reassurance — it's an admission that the people steering LIV are as much in the dark as everyone else. When a two-time major champion says infrastructure for a marquee team event still doesn't exist with weeks to go, that's not a scheduling hiccup, that's a league that stopped planning ahead months ago.

The Asian Tour defection stings worse than the Michigan collapse long-term. Losing a venue is embarrassing. Losing your only promotion pathway to a rival alliance that includes the PGA Tour and DP World Tour is a structural wound. Kaymer's comment about being told in February the league was funded through 2031, only to watch that unravel within two months, says everything about how fragile LIV's financial footing really is. If the September funding deadline comes and goes without a signed check, there won't be a 2027 conversation to have.
