Scottie Scheffler Will Play in the U.S. Open Final Pairing on His Birthday

World No. 1 Scheffler plays the final group on his 30th birthday, chasing a career Grand Slam. Can he overturn a historic six-shot deficit at Shinnecock?

BY RAWGOLF STAFF PUBLISHED SUN 21 JUN

Quick answer: Scottie Scheffler, the world No. 1, earned a spot in the final pairing at the 2026 U.S. Open alongside leader Wyndham Clark. He gets there on a day that doubles as his 30th birthday and Father's Day. A win would complete the career Grand Slam and make him only the seventh man ever to do it. The catch: he starts six shots back, a deficit handed over just once in major history.

It is the best story in golf and the longest of long shots, all wrapped into one Sunday afternoon. Here is everything at stake.

Why Is Scheffler in the Final Pairing?

He earned it the hard way, on a brutal day. Saturday at Shinnecock was a bloodbath, with only two players in the entire field breaking par. Scheffler was one of them. His 1-under 69 was a grind-it-out masterclass that climbed him into a tie for second and, because he was first to post that number, slotted him into the last tee time next to Clark.

What makes it more impressive is how it started. Scheffler opened with back-to-back bogeys, including a drive into what he called the worst lie he had seen all week, a ball he could only advance 48 yards. Most players let a start like that snowball. He steadied himself, ground out seven straight pars, then caught fire on the back nine with a run of three straight birdies, the first time he has ever done that in a U.S. Open round. He fought his way into the final group instead of fading out of the tournament.

What Is at Stake for Scheffler on Sunday?

The career Grand Slam. The whole thing.

Scheffler has already won the Masters, the PGA Championship, and The Open Championship. The U.S. Open is the one major that has never had his name on it. Win at Shinnecock and he joins one of the most exclusive clubs in the sport, becoming just the seventh golfer in history to claim all four. The other six are Gene Sarazen, Ben Hogan, Gary Player, Jack Nicklaus, Tiger Woods, and Rory McIlroy, who completed his own slam last year. That is the company on offer.

And the timing borders on absurd. He would do it on his 30th birthday, on Father's Day. Only two players have ever finished the slam before turning 30: Woods at 24 and Nicklaus at 26. Scheffler is right on the edge of that window. Get it done Sunday and he sneaks in under the wire with the legends.

How Big Is the Deficit He Has to Overcome?

Six shots. And that number is the whole problem.

A six-stroke hole in the final round of a major is enormous, and the history is bleak for anyone chasing. Twenty-one players have taken a lead that size or larger into the last round of a major. Twenty of them won. The only one who did not was Greg Norman, who melted down with a 78 at the 1996 Masters and handed the green jacket to Nick Faldo. So the odds give Scheffler roughly a one-in-twenty path, and even that depends partly on Clark cooperating.

There is also Scheffler's own record to weigh. The largest 54-hole deficit he has ever come back from to win is five shots, at the 2024 PLAYERS Championship. He is being asked to top his career-best comeback, against the best front-runner in golf, on a course that punishes every loose swing. Doable on paper. Very hard in reality.

Can He Actually Catch Wyndham Clark?

Realistically, he needs two things to happen at once: a special round from himself and a wobble from Clark.

The first part is in his hands. Scheffler is the best player in the world for a reason, and if anyone can post a low number on a hard course under pressure, it is him. The second part is not in his control at all. Clark has led after every single round this week, putted like a machine, and shown zero signs of cracking. He also already owns a U.S. Open title, so the moment will not swallow him.

The honest read is that Scheffler probably needs Clark to get nervous, because six shots do not vanish on talent alone. The good news for the birthday boy is that he gets to apply that pressure directly, eyeball to eyeball, in the same group. If he starts fast and the lead shrinks to three or four through nine holes, Shinnecock's back nine and the weight of expectation could do the rest. That is the only script that works.

What Would a Birthday Win Mean?

It would be one of the great single days in modern golf. A career Grand Slam, completed on a 30th birthday, on Father's Day, in the final group, by erasing a six-shot deficit that has only been overturned once in the history of the majors. If you pitched that as a movie, an editor would tell you to tone it down.

Even Scheffler has tried to keep the temperature low. He said before the week that the grand slam has never been a motivating factor for him, and he cracked jokes about his birthday landing on Father's Day. But the stakes are obvious, and he knows it. He spent his Saturday press conference talking about "stealing" shots from a course that gave up almost nothing, which tells you exactly how hard he is having to fight just to stay in range.

The Raw Read

Strip away the fairy tale and the math is unkind. Scheffler is a massive underdog Sunday, chasing a player who has not flinched all week. The smart money says Clark lifts the trophy and Scheffler spends his 30th birthday as the runner-up.

But here is why people will watch anyway. Golf is the one sport where the leader has to come to you, where a six-shot lead is technically safe and emotionally terrifying, and where the ghost of 1996 sits in the back of every front-runner's mind. Scheffler does not need to play perfect. He needs to play great, get hot early, and let the pressure do the part he cannot. Long odds are not no odds.

Whatever happens, the setup alone is something special: the best player alive, in the final group, on his birthday, one round from immortality. The result is still being written. The story is already worth it.
