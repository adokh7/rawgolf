Fleetwood Got Within One. Then Birkdale Turned

Tommy Fleetwood got within one shot of The Open lead. Two late bogeys left Southport dreaming—and five shots back.

BY COLIN HAYES PUBLISHED: TUE 21 JUL 2026
The Quick Verdict

Tommy Fleetwood got within one shot of the lead during round three of the 2026 Open Championship, but finished five behind Sam Burns after a one-under 69 at Royal Birkdale. Burns held the 54-hole lead at 10 under, with Si Woo Kim and Ryan Fox two back; Fleetwood was five under after bogeys at the 15th and 18th.

That is the real version of the story. Fleetwood had his moment—properly had it. Then the golf course took a bite, and the fairytale acquired some teeth.

How close did Fleetwood get?

Fleetwood moved to within one stroke of the lead when he knocked his approach at the 11th to about two feet. The crowd around Royal Birkdale erupted, because this was not just any English player having a good Saturday; this was Southport’s own man threatening to take over The Open.

He had already made two birdies in three holes in the middle of the front nine. Then came the shot into No. 11. Two feet. One behind. You could hear the place shaking.

Fleetwood grew up in Southport and, as a kid, would sneak onto Royal Birkdale late in the evening because he could not afford to play it. That is why the noise felt different. This was not manufactured home support with branded flags and a hospitality tent. These were people watching a local boy get close to the thing they all wanted for him.

For a while, you could almost believe the course was going to cooperate.

It did not.

What cost Fleetwood on Saturday?

The 15th and 18th holes cost Fleetwood two bogeys over his final four holes, turning a one-shot chase into a five-shot deficit. At the 15th, he missed the green down the steep right-side slope, played an excellent pitch, then missed a six-foot par putt.

That is Open golf in a nutshell. The poor miss hurts. The recovery gives you a way out. Then the short putt says, not today.

At the 18th, Fleetwood found a pot bunker. He had to lay up, then fought hard enough to make bogey. That sounds harmless written down. It was not harmless. Burns was already in the clubhouse at 10 under. Fleetwood’s bogey meant he signed for 69 at five under overall.

Five back.

There is a huge difference between walking off the 18th two or three behind and walking off five behind. The scoreboard does not care about momentum, local affection or childhood memories. It just adds them up.

You can call those two holes “missed opportunities” if you like. That is polite language for golf writers with clean shoes. They were expensive mistakes.

Who led after round three?

Sam Burns led The Open at 10 under after posting a five-under 65, two clear of Si Woo Kim and Ryan Fox. Burns followed Friday’s record-tying 62 with the best kind of moving-day round: low, controlled, and good enough to make everyone else chase.

Burns had opened the championship with a 73. Now he was 10 under through 54 holes. That is a useful reminder before you start writing off anybody after Thursday at an Open. The tournament does not care when you get hot. It only cares whether you are hot when the weekend gets serious.

Burns was not even supposed to be there, at least not originally. He changed his mind about playing The Open a week after the birth of his daughter and flew from Louisiana. Two weeks later, he was leading the oldest major in the sport.

Golf loves that sort of story. Until Sunday asks for payment.

Were DeChambeau and Scheffler still alive?

Yes. Bryson DeChambeau and Scottie Scheffler remained in contention, but neither owned the Saturday the way Burns did. The PGA TOUR’s round-three coverage identified both as part of the late-day chase while Burns took command.

That is as far as the reliable round-three reporting lets us go here. The scorecards matter, but the bigger point is easier to see: neither player did enough on Saturday to turn the championship into their personal stage.

DeChambeau had already spent the week carrying the baggage of a two-shot rules penalty and the circus that followed it. Scheffler had the game to scare anybody, as always, but not the kind of Saturday surge that sends a final group looking over its shoulder every hole.

And Fleetwood? He had the greatest crowd on the property and a real chance to make Sunday feel like a coronation. Instead, he had work to do.

What did Sunday require from Fleetwood?

Fleetwood needed an aggressive final round and help from Burns, because five shots separated them after three rounds. His opening Sunday birdie from 73 feet showed the crowd was not done believing.

That putt was not a solution. It was a flare in the sky.

You cannot plan a Sunday comeback around 73-foot birdie putts. You need early pressure, clean drives, sharp iron play and a leader who starts feeling the weight of each shot. You need the kind of day where the gallery gets louder with every hole and the man in front starts hearing it.

Fleetwood had the support. He had a course he knew in his bones. The question was whether he could get close enough before Burns made the whole thing boring.

The Raw Take

Fleetwood’s Saturday was not a collapse. Calling it that would be lazy.

It was worse in a way. He played well enough to get the entire town dreaming, got within one shot of the lead, then watched two ordinary Open mistakes put the leader five shots away.

That is the cruelty of major golf. You do not always get knocked out with a disaster. Sometimes you play a good round, miss a six-footer, find one bunker, and walk off wondering how the whole bloody thing got so far away.
